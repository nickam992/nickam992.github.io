1.1 The Characteristics of Life

Biology is the study of life. All living organisms share common characteristics:

  • They have levels of organization—atoms, molecules, cells, tissues, organs, organ systems, organisms, populations, community, ecosystem, and biosphere.

  • They acquire materials and energy from the environment. Metabolism is the sum of the reactions involved in these processes. Photosynthesis, which occurs in organisms such as plants, is responsible for producing the organic molecules that serve as food for most organisms.

  • They reproduce and experience growth, and in many cases development.

  • They maintain homeostasis to maintain the conditions of an internal environment.

  • They respond to stimuli.

  • They have an evolutionary history and are adapted to a way of life.

1.2 Humans Are Related to Other Animals

The classification of living organisms mirrors their evolutionary relationships. Humans are mammals, a type of vertebrate in the animal kingdom of the domain Eukarya. Humans differ from other mammals, including apes, by their

  • Highly developed brains

  • Completely upright stance

  • Creative language

  • Ability to use a wide variety of tools

Humans Have a Cultural Heritage

Language, tool use, values, and information are passed on from one generation to the next.

Humans Are Members of the Biosphere

Humans depend on the biosphere for its many services, such as absorption of pollutants, sources of water and food, prevention of soil erosion, and natural beauty. Human population growth and use of resources threaten the biodiversity of the biosphere and is leading to the extinction of many species.

1.3 Science as a Process

Science is a way of knowing about the natural world. The scientific method consists of

  • Making an observation

  • Formulating a hypothesis using inductive reasoning

  • Determining an experimental design to test the hypothesis using a series of experiments. The experiment contains a variable and an experimental variable, as well as a test group and control group. A placebo may be used in the control group to limit the variables in the experiments. Models, such as model organisms, may be used to control the experiment.

  • Making a prediction as to the outcome of the experiment

  • Collecting and analyzing the data from the experiment

  • Coming to a conclusion based on the analysis of the data. Over time, these conclusions, if accepted by the scientific community, may become theories, principles, or laws. 

  • Presenting the results of the study for peer review in a scientific journal.

1.4 Making Sense of a Scientific Study

In general, when assessing a scientific study you should beware of anecdotal and correlational data, because they need further study to be substantiated. When examining graphs, ensure that they clearly show the relationship between quantities.

Statistical Data

The standard error tells us how uncertain a particular value is. The statistical significance tells us how trustworthy the results are. The lower the statistical significance, the less likely the results are due to chance and the more likely they are due to the experimental variable.

1.5 Challenges Facing Science

  • Scientific findings often lead to the development of a technology that can be of service to human beings. Technologies have both benefits and drawbacks. Bioethics is concerned with the consequences of using biological technology.

  • There are a number of important issues facing science in today’s world. These include emerging diseases; human influence on ecosystems, which is resulting in a loss of biodiversity and extinction; and global warming, which is contributing to climate change.
